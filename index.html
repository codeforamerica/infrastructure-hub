  1. Welcome to ATL Vision 2015!

    Mayor Kasim Reed has made a commitment to address the city's pressing infrastructure challenges. Today, the city faces an infrastructure backlog of more than $900 million. In order to begin improving our city's roads, bridges, sidewalks and expand our green spaces, the city is proposing a bond infrastructure referendum to pay for these repairs and improvements. The Departments of Public Works, Planning and Community Development, in coordination with the Atlanta City Council, are seeking public input to determine priority and criteria for project selection for the bond.

    Citizen input is a very important part of this process, and this website has been to give citizens a place to provide feedback throughout all phases of the project. The city will use this feedback to determine project selection criteria, narrow down the list of final projects, and to update citizens on progress.

  2. What is an infrastructure bond?

    Bonds are investment opportunities issued by an entity that is authorized to sell bonds to investors. After the bonds are released into the market and investors subscribe to them, the proceeds are used to finance infrastructure projects. These developments include basic facilities and other projects. The facilities may include transportation and communication systems, and public buildings.

    An Infrastructure Bond Referendum is a vote by registered voters within the City of Atlanta to approve or reject a request from the City to borrow money to fund infrastructure projects.

    Why do an Infrastructure Bond?

    Atlanta has aging transportation infrastructure

    $881M is needed to restore and/or replace existing bridges, roads, sidewalks, street lights, signals and signs to good condition.

    Atlanta has aging facility infrastructure

    $130.8M is needed to restore and/or replace city facilities (fire stations, police stations, recreation centers, etc.) to good condition.

    The decision will rest with the voters

    The City of Atlanta recommends a diversified funding plan to address the infrastructure backlog that begins with a $250M general obligation bond to be presented to the voters in March 2015.

  3. How will the infrastructure bond be funded?

    The debt service on the bonds, estimated at $17 million annually, will be paid through revenue generated from operational efficiencies identified by the Mayor’s Blue Ribbon Commission on Waste & Efficiency in Government.

    Atlanta taxpayers will see no increase in their property or sales taxes as a result of the infrastructure bond.

  4. What kinds of projects will be included?

    The infrastructure bond will focus first and foremost on critical public safety improvements such as bridge replacement and maintenance, street resurfacing, ADA accessibility, and facility upgrades. In total, there are ten (10) categories of infrastructure projects that will be part of the bond measure, including six types of transportation projects and four types of facilities projects.

    The bond is limited to infrastructure related to general government operations, therefore excluding the City’s water/sewer infrastructure and the infrastructure at Hartsfield-Jackson Atlanta International Airport that receives funding through independent enterprise funds. For facilities, only properties owned outright by the city are included. Leased facilities are excluded from the bond because it is the responsibility of the owners to maintain the properties.

    Project Categories: Transportation Infrastructure

    1. TIA Approved Projects

    These are projects approved by citywide vote as part of the 2010 Transportation Investment Act.

    2. Bridges

    The City of Atlanta is responsible for maintenance of 164 bridges. The safety of these bridges is rated by GDOT, who perform a biannual inspection of bridges and assign Sufficiency Ratings for the bridges. Any bridge with a sufficiency rating below 50 is considered to be a candidate for major repairs or replacement. Any bridge considered unsafe according to these metrics is immediately closed to traffic.

    3. Streets and ADA Ramps

    Street resurfacing consists of either micro-surfacing/crack sealing or milling and overlaying roadways. There are five categories of streets: Arterial, Collector, Residential, Industrial, and Unpaved.

    Furthermore, the city has over 30,000 intersection nodes requiring ADA ramp improvements. DPW is required to install adequate curb ramps at sidewalk locations of all streets resurfaced since 1992.

    4. Sidewalks and Curbing

    Although new sidewalks have been installed under the Quality of Life Bond Program much of Atlanta's 2158 miles of sidewalk are past their lifecycle of fifty years, particularly in central Atlanta, where many sidewalks are paved with hexagonal concrete and brick pavers. These sidewalks have deteriorated, and do not meet ADA requirements. Curbing, often constructed of rock or deteriorating concrete, providing insufficient stormwater control.

    5. Lights, Signals and Signs

    Upgrades to streetlights, traffic signals, and signs are critical to improving traffic circulation and energy efficiency in Atlanta. Street light improvements are aimed at replacing deteriorating and knocked-down lights, as well as reducing energy costs by incorporating LED lighting. Traffic signal upgrades will establish remote communication to ATCC and so signals can be adjusted remotely to react to traffic and emergencies (such as a city-wide evacuation). Finally, signage in Atlanta must abide by the MUTCD standards for the installation and placement of traffic signs on public roads. These standards include minimum reflectivity, readability, and placement guidelines.

    6. School Zone Flashers

    The primary purpose for school zone flashers is to reduce the speed of vehicles in a school zone, in an effort to provide a safer environment for school children. Of 110 school zone flashers in Atlanta, 52 are past their lifecycle.
