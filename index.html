  2. What is the 2015 infrastructure bond?

    Atlanta’s infrastructure is aging, which presents challenges for both public safety and maintenance. In fact, as of mid-2014, the city has identified an infrastructure backlog of over 1 billion dollars.

    This backlog consists of $881 million dollars worth of transportation projects, $41 million of fleet upgrades and replacements, and $131 million dollars worth of facility projects. Of this billion-dollar backlog, approximately $305 million (combined) is considered high priority.

    In an effort to reduce this backlog and invest in making Atlanta an even better place to live, work, bike, walk and drive, the city hopes to issue a $250 million dollar municipal bond dedicated to both transportation and facility infrastructure.

  3. How will the infrastructure bond be funded?

    This bond will NOT raise taxes for citizens. Instead, it will be funded through a combination of cost savings and revenue generation in the FY2015 budget. Mayor Kasim Reed recently appointed a Commission on Waste and Efficiency in Government to identify potential cost savings areas, and the city’s Innovation Delivery and Performance Team is also focused on areas where the city can generate new revenue streams.

    Depending on the cost savings and revenue identified, the infrastructure bond may exceed $250 million, meaning that the city will be able to implement even more important projects for the public. However, even if the bond amount increases, the bond will NOT increase taxes.

  4. What kinds of projects will be included?

    This bond focuses first and foremost on critical public safety improvements such as bridge replacement and maintenance, street resurfacing, ADA accessibility, and facility upgrades. In total, there are nine categories of infrastructure projects that will be part of the bond measure, including six types of transportation projects and three types of facilities projects.

    The bond is limited to infrastructure related to general government operations, therefore excluding the City’s water/sewer infrastructure and the infrastructure at Hartsfield-Jackson Atlanta International Airport that receive funding through independent enterprise funds. For facilities, only properties owned outright by the city are included. Leased facilities are excluded from the bond because it is the responsibility of the owners to maintain the properties.

    Project Categories: Transportation Infrastructure

    1. TIA Approved Projects

    These are projects approved by citywide vote as part of the 2010 Transportation Investment Act.

    2. Bridges

    The City of Atlanta is responsible for maintenance of 164 bridges. The safety of these bridges is rated by GDOT, who perform a biannual inspection of bridges and assign Sufficiency Ratings for the bridges. Any bridge with a sufficiency rating below 50 is considered to be a candidate for major repairs or replacement. Any bridge considered unsafe according to these metrics is immediately closed to traffic.

    3. Streets and ADA Ramps

    Street resurfacing consists of either micro-surfacing/crack sealing or milling and overlaying roadways. There are five categories of streets: Arterial, Collector, Residential, Industrial, and Unpaved.

    Furthermore, the city has over 30,000 intersection nodes requiring ADA ramp improvements. DPW is required to install adequate curb ramps at sidewalk locations of all streets resurfaced since 1992.

    4. Sidewalks and Curbing

    Although new sidewalks have been installed under the Quality of Life Bond Program much of Atlanta's 2158 miles of sidewalk are past their lifecycle of fifty years, particularly in central Atlanta, where many sidewalks are paved with hexagonal concrete and brick pavers. These sidewalks have deteriorated, and do not meet ADA requirements. Curbing, often constructed of rock or deteriorating concrete, providing insufficient stormwater control.

    5. Lights, Signals and Signs

    Upgrades to streetlights, traffic signals, and signs are critical to improving traffic circulation and energy efficiency in Atlanta. Street light improvements are aimed at replacing deteriorating and knocked-down lights, as well as reducing energy costs by incorporating LED lighting. Traffic signal upgrades will establish remote communication to ATCC and so signals can be adjusted remotely to react to traffic and emergencies (such as a city-wide evacuation). Finally, signage in Atlanta must abide by the MUTCD standards for the installation and placement of traffic signs on public roads. These standards include minimum reflectivity, readability, and placement guidelines.

    6. School Zone Flashers

    The primary purpose for school zone flashers is to reduce the speed of vehicles in a school zone, in an effort to provide a safer environment for school children. Of 110 school zone flashers in Atlanta, 52 are past their lifecycle.
